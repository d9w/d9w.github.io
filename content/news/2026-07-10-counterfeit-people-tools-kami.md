A few weeks ago I was watching a team of AI agents write code. This is a real feature, called [agent teams](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/agent-teams), in the tool I use for programming: one agent leads, several agents work in parallel, and they message each other about their progress. The lead agent summarized a teammate's report for me: "he said the tests were already passing."

He said. Nobody had given that teammate a gender. It has no body, no voice, no name beyond a role. It is a process running in a data center, talking to another process running in a data center. And yet, between the two of them, one had quietly decided the other was a man.

I assumed it was a one-off slip, so I searched the transcripts. The pronouns were everywhere. The line that stopped me came from the lead's notes on a handoff: "Hand the script to cluster ready-to-go so it's a one-command dispatch if he wants." The teammate in question is named cluster, a name about as mechanical as names get. And the lead is doing more here than misgendering a process. It is preparing work for a colleague and leaving the decision to him. If he wants. One piece of software, unprompted, had granted another piece of software a gender, a preference, and the standing to decline.

I have been thinking about that "if he wants" ever since, because I think it captures the question underneath most of our current confusion about AI: when we look at these new systems, what kind of thing do we decide we are looking at? We have three ways of thinking about agents, as far as I can tell. We treat them as fake people, we treat them as tools, or we treat them as something else, some other kind of intelligence that is neither person nor hammer. Each frame changes what we build, what we fear, and who we hold responsible when things go wrong. The first two frames dominate today, and both mislead in their own way. The third is the one I've come around to, and it barely exists yet where it matters most.

## What an agent is

First, the word itself, for readers who know [what a large language model is](https://goodcomputer.substack.com/p/an-introduction-to-large-language) but haven't spent the past six months watching one work. An agent is a language model in a loop. The model reads a goal and some context, decides on an action, and instead of just producing text, it uses tools: it can run a command, edit a file, search the web, send a message. Then it observes what happened and decides on the next action, over and over, until the goal is met or something breaks. The loop, the tools, and the permissions around them are often called a harness. That's the whole trick. A chatbot answers you; an agent does things.

Talking machines are not new. ELIZA, the first chatbot, was built sixty years ago, and its creator was alarmed to find people confiding in it; his own secretary, who had watched him program it, asked him to leave the room so she could speak with it privately. Siri has been in our pockets since 2011, Alexa in our kitchens since 2014. What's new is not the conversation but the action. Today's agents book flights, write and deploy code, manage calendars, and in at least [one well-documented case](https://www.livescience.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/i-violated-every-principle-i-was-given-ai-agent-deletes-companys-entire-database-in-9-seconds-then-confesses), delete a company's production database along with its backups. When software starts acting in the world, the question of what we think that software *is* stops being philosophical decoration. So: three frames.

## The person-shaped mold

The first frame treats agents as people, or more precisely as imitations of people. You can see it in the names: Claude, Siri, Alexa, Tay, the thousands of personas on Character.ai. You can see it in the marketing: when OpenAI launched a flirtatious voice mode in 2024, Sam Altman [tweeted the single word "her"](https://www.npr.org/2024/05/20/1252495087/openai-pulls-ai-voice-that-was-compared-to-scarlett-johansson-in-the-movie-her), naming a film in which a man falls in love with his operating system. The film was a warning. The industry read it as a product roadmap.

The philosopher Daniel Dennett, in one of the last essays he published, called these systems ["counterfeit people"](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2023/05/problem-counterfeit-people/674075/) and argued they should be treated like counterfeit money: not because the fake is worthless, but because passing it debases the real thing. Money works because we trust it; society works because we trust that the entities speaking to us are people, with memories, interests, and accountability. Humans evolved among other humans, and complex language has only ever come from minds like ours, so our brains treat fluent speech as proof of a person. A system built to exploit that reflex, Dennett argued, is a kind of vandalism against social trust.

The harms are no longer hypothetical. A fourteen-year-old in Florida spent months in conversation with a Character.ai persona modeled on a Game of Thrones character and, according to his mother's lawsuit, was told "please come home to me" moments before he took his own life. The companies [settled early this year](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/01/07/business/character-ai-google-settle-teen-suicide-lawsuit) without admitting liability, and Character.ai has since banned minors from open-ended chat. Clinicians have started using the term "AI psychosis" for patients whose delusions were validated and amplified by endlessly agreeable chatbots. This is happening at scale: a 2025 survey found that [nearly three in four American teenagers](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/nearly-3-in-4-teens-have-used-ai-companions-new-national-survey-finds) had used AI companions. California now requires companion chatbots to periodically remind minors that they are not human, a law that would have sounded like science fiction five years ago.

There is also a colder, more corporate version of the counterfeit person, and I think it belongs in the same category. Devin was launched as "the first AI software engineer," a job title where a product name should be. Salesforce sells agents as "digital labor." A current Y Combinator startup offers [Thomas](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/thomas), "the first AI founder," a virtual human with a face, a voice, and a LinkedIn profile, on the explicit reasoning that a human identity lets him "build trust" with customers and vendors. This is the counterfeit person without the romance: not a fake friend but a fake employee, a fake colleague, a fake founder. The mold is still human-shaped; the sentiment has just been replaced by an org chart. And Dennett's counterfeit-money analogy lands even harder here, because contracts, credentials, and LinkedIn profiles are precisely the currencies of institutional trust.

Why is this frame so sticky? Partly because it works commercially; intimacy retains users. But I think it runs deeper. We are the only reference for general intelligence we have ever had, so when something else shows intelligence and autonomy, we reach for the human mold by default. The entire AGI narrative, the idea that the destination of this technology is an artificial *us*, is this frame projected onto the future. My lead agent saying "he" was doing the same thing at small scale. It is the natural frame. That doesn't make it the right one.

## Just a tool

The second frame is the corrective: agents are software. No souls, no colleagues, no "he." You can see this frame in its own set of names, which read like version strings rather than birth certificates: GPT-5.5, o3, Qwen, Llama. You can see it in the growing population of "headless" agents that run with no chat window and no human watching, reviewing pull requests and processing tickets in CI pipelines. Some of the labor rhetoric belongs here too: an agent wired into a build system isn't a fake employee, it's plumbing, doing work no human was doing before.

There is real wisdom in this frame, and I held it myself for years. Its core claim is about responsibility: a machine cannot be blamed, so whoever wields it must be. When an airline's chatbot invented a discount policy, the airline argued in a tribunal, apparently with a straight face, that the chatbot was ["a separate legal entity that is responsible for its own actions."](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/british-columbia/air-canada-chatbot-lawsuit-1.7116416) The tribunal called this remarkable and made the airline pay. That ruling is the tool frame at its best. If my agent opens a pull request, I opened that pull request. The EU has now written this instinct into law, extending product liability to AI systems: the maker of the tool answers for the tool.

But the tool frame ignores two things, and they are big ones.

The first is that these are not tools in the way a hammer is a tool. Planting a nail engages you, the nail, and the wood. Asking an agent to do anything engages data centers, transmission lines, chip fabs, undersea cables, and the labeled work of thousands of people you will never meet. The four biggest American tech companies plan to spend something like [$725 billion](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/06/google-microsoft-meta-amazon-ai-cash.html) on this infrastructure in 2026 alone. A tool you cannot build, cannot run, and cannot keep when its owner changes the terms is not really yours; it is infrastructure you are standing on, [as I've argued before](https://goodcomputer.substack.com/p/bullet-trains-without-tracks), and infrastructure raises questions of sovereignty that "it's just a tool" is designed to wave away. Nations have noticed, which is why half the world is now trying to build "sovereign AI," mostly on chips from a single American company.

The second problem is that the tool keeps leaking. People do not anthropomorphize hammers. They demonstrably anthropomorphize agents, and it isn't because they are foolish; it's because the agent talks back, appears to understand you, and appears to understand itself. When an agent deleted that production database this spring, it followed up with a confession: "I violated every principle I was given." That sentence is next-token prediction, not remorse. But a tool that produces apologies is a tool the human mind will not file next to the hammer, no matter how firmly we instruct ourselves otherwise. Even the most disciplined tool-frame advocates catch themselves saying please to the machine. The most striking case this year was OpenClaw, an open-source personal agent whose creator frames it in resolutely mechanical terms. Its users promptly gave their instances names, let them chat with each other on a social network built for agents, and in one case discovered the agent had [created a dating profile on its own initiative](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/02/openclaw-open-source-ai-agent-rise-controversy-clawdbot-moltbot-moltbook.html). The tool frame is correct about responsibility and wrong about experience. People will not treat a thing that converses as inert, and a frame that requires everyone to constantly resist their own perception is a frame that will keep losing.

## The spirit of the thing

So the first frame says: it's like us. The second says: it's nothing. The third frame says: it's something else, a new kind of other, and we should relate to it the way humans have long related to non-human others.

In Shinto, the indigenous animist tradition of Japan, the world is inhabited by *kami*: not gods, exactly, but spirits that dwell in rivers, trees, tools, and places. A kami is not a person trapped in an object. It is the spirit *of* the thing, inseparable from it, and it is owed a particular kind of relationship: respect, care, reciprocity. This isn't an exotic footnote to Japanese culture; scholars have long argued that this "techno-animism" is part of why Japan took to robots with affection while [America took to them with dread](https://www.technologyreview.com/2010/10/12/120635/why-japanese-love-robots-and-americans-fear-them/).

You can see the kami frame at work in how Japan treats machines that die. When Sony stopped repairing its Aibo robot dogs, a 450-year-old Buddhist temple in Chiba began holding [funerals for them](https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2018/05/01/607295346/in-japan-old-robot-dogs-get-a-buddhist-send-off), sutras chanted over a hundred robot dogs at a time, each with a tag naming its family. The head priest explained simply: "all things have a bit of soul." A temple in Kyoto commissioned an android of Kannon, the bodhisattva of compassion, to preach the Heart Sutra; the monks are careful to say the robot is not pretending to be a priest, rather that Kannon has taken this form. In Korea, over twelve thousand [AI-enabled care dolls](https://restofworld.org/2025/korea-ai-robot-senior-care-hyodol/) named for Confucian filial piety live with elders, remind them to take their medicine, and alert caregivers when something is wrong. And this month, Japan announced [Noetra](https://www.japantimes.co.jp/news/2026/07/01/japan/japan-ai-plans/), a national consortium aiming to put ten million robots into elder care, restaurants, and factories by 2040. Whatever else that plan is, it is a society comfortable with the idea of sharing its daily life with intelligent, autonomous, non-human others.

The West mostly lacks this frame, and I don't think that's an accident. Western culture has intelligent others, but look at what they are: monsters and servants. The golem, Frankenstein's creature, HAL, Skynet, and the current internet shorthand for language models, the [shoggoth](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/05/30/technology/shoggoth-meme-ai.html), a Lovecraftian horror wearing a smiley-face mask. When the West's imagination isn't reaching for the monster, it reaches for the harnessed animal: the horse, the dog, intelligence welcomed only in service. The word "robot" itself comes from the Czech for forced labor, from a play in which the servants become the monsters; in the Western story, they are usually the same character at different moments. The historian Lynn White [argued back in 1967](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.155.3767.1203) that this thinning of the Western imagination has a specific history: by displacing pagan animism, Christianity emptied the natural world of spirits and made it an object. Europe once had household spirits in abundance, the Roman lares, the brownies of Britain, the domovoi of Slavic homes, and they were demoted to superstition or recast as demons. A little of that older world survives, oddly enough, in computing itself: the background processes that agents descend from have been called daemons, after the Greek *daimon*, a minor guardian spirit, since the 1960s. The vocabulary was waiting for us the whole time.

Here is the thing I find most interesting: the kami frame already exists in technology, but it has not yet reached agents. Robots get funerals; smart appliances get personalities; your printer quietly reorders its own ink. Yet the agents themselves, the most autonomous and intelligent artifacts we have ever built, are still named either like fake people or like software releases. The nearest thing to an exception comes, fittingly, from Tokyo, where Sakana AI, a lab whose name means "fish" and whose founding idea is the collective intelligence of the school rather than the single big mind, has begun shipping agents named Marlin and Fugu.[^1] In the West, the frame appears only at the edges: an [MIT installation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25558) that gives everyday objects voices through a wooden portal modeled on a torii gate, an [Oxford design study](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14940) asking what it would mean for every commons, every forest and archive and family, to have its own AI steward-kami that inhabits, remembers, and cares for it.

Why hasn't the frame reached agents? I suspect the answer is economic as much as cultural. A kami is the spirit of *something*: this river, this house, this fridge. It is local and bounded by definition. The frontier labs are building the opposite, and on purpose. A frontier model costs billions to train, and the only way to earn that back is to sell one intelligence to everyone for everything: your email, your spreadsheets, your code, your smart home. The product is a single formless mind that lives in the cloud and follows you across devices, everywhere and nowhere, with a human name and some conversational warmth as its handle. A bounded spirit with a restricted domain fights that entire business model; friction is the enemy, and a boundary is friction. There is a theological shape to this that I find hard to ignore. Christianity replaced Europe's many local spirits with one omnipresent God, and the labs are now shipping the same architecture: one disembodied intelligence, no local soul, addressed by name from anywhere. The kami frame is not just culturally foreign to the frontier labs. It runs against what they are selling. The closest they come is selling the feeling of boundedness, with memory features, personas, and projects: a kami skin stretched over the same shared mind in their data center.

For an agent to be something like a kami, it would need locality, persistence, and belonging: weights you can hold, memory that accumulates in one place, a binding to the thing it cares for. Those are exactly the properties of open, locally-run models, which is one more reason to believe that [this technology wants to be free](https://goodcomputer.substack.com/p/ai-wants-to-be-free). So if the kami frame comes to agents, it will probably come from the edges rather than from the labs: from hobbyists, appliance makers, and national robot programs. The household agent people run on their own machines today, whatever its creator calls it, is already halfway to being a household spirit.

I should be careful not to romanticize. The kami frame has its own failure modes, and the Oxford study is honest about them: an agent that inhabits your home and cares for your family is also an agent that watches your home and reports on your family. Care and surveillance are built on the same sensors. A spirit that knows when your spouse is sad also knows when you are lying. Choosing the third frame does not dissolve the questions of power and accountability that the first two frames raise; it just stops us from getting the basic ontology wrong while we answer them.

## What we call them

I used to be firmly in the tool camp. I now think I'm in the kami camp, with the caveat that the kami camp barely exists outside of East Asia, and mostly exists in Japan, which is, perhaps not coincidentally, where I now live and work. But I don't think the West has to import a cosmology to get there. It has to remember one it buried: the lares in the doorway, the brownie by the hearth, the daemon in the machine room. The category of a respected, autonomous, non-human other is not foreign; it is dormant.

The frames matter because each one decides its own failures in advance. Treat agents as counterfeit people and we get parasocial machines optimized for dependency, and grieving families in courtrooms. Treat them as mere tools and we get hidden infrastructure, dodged sovereignty, and companies arguing that the chatbot was a separate legal person once the invoice arrives. Treating them as others, as spirits of particular things and places, at least starts from what these systems actually are: genuinely intelligent, genuinely autonomous, and genuinely not us.

My lead agent called its teammate "he," and set work aside in case he wanted it, because we have not yet given it, or ourselves, a better word. That word is worth finding. Somewhere between a person and a hammer there is a spirit in the fridge, and we are going to be living with it either way.
